The visual and interaction design for the family dashboard — the look, the feel, the motion, and how a person navigates it. This is the design source of truth.

## Nomenclature

- The Wall — the whole thing. The tile surface on the 43″ panel. ("The Wall is showing the game." "Put the cameras on the Wall.") Replaces the generic word "dashboard" in design conversation.
- Tiles/Cards — the individual boards/panels on the Wall. (Windows Phone called these Live Tiles; ours are live too.) Replaces "boards" and "cards/panels" as the unit of design.
- The Stage — the top, main 4x3 viewing window. WYSIWYG.
- The List/Scroll List — the unused 1x1 tiles underneath The Stage.
- The Gutter — the near black area around/above/beneath the tiles.
- Domino — any 1x2 or 2x1 tile.
- Face — A side of a tile that displays information.
- Cascade — The animation that reorganizes tiles on the stage to prevent burn-in and to provide nice ambient visuals.
- Closed Tile — A tile in its 1x1 size.
- Open Tile — A tile not in its 1x1 size, any size other than 1x1.
- Design language — Windows Phone Metro: flat, typographic, bold colour blocks, content-forward, tiles that flip.

## Core concept: a tile deck, not navigation

The Wall is not organized by room and not organized as hidden navigation (no bottom-nav-only, no master-detail rail). It is a deck of tiles drawn from a library, sized and arranged on a grid, where tiles are equally about data (markets, weather, sports, traffic) and home automation (cameras, lights, climate). A tile is simply a thing worth watching or controlling — its category is irrelevant to the structure. Chosen deliberately: the owner cares about data as much as automation, so a room-based or automation-only structure is the wrong fit.

## The shape

- Grid — 4 columns × 3 rows = 12 cells.
- Tile sizes — 1×1, 1×2, 2×2, 2x3, 4×3 and any inverse excluding 4x3. (4×3 = the entire grid, i.e. full-Wall takeover / single-view mode). Tile size is how a tile goes big — no separate full-screen mode; 4×3 is full-screen.
- Library of at least 12 tiles (11 + Settings, folded into Home).
- Home tile — ambient content (clock, date). Not a fixed anchor: Home drifts with cascade, which helps burn-in (no tile is permanently fixed). Navigation does not depend on finding Home — see the drawer model below.
- STAGE + SCROLLING LIST. The 4×3 is the stage — the 16:9 panel, the calm subset, the only place a tile opens, and always 12 cells full. Every other tile lives just below as a 1×1 in a vertically-scrolling drawer; scroll down to browse the whole library, and the scroll snaps to whole 4×3 screens (never rests mid-tile). With 12 tiles the drawer is ≤1 screen. Opening a drawer tile sorts it up onto the stage; if the stage can't fit it, the oldest open tile demotes to 1×1. (The always-full / eviction mechanics are enforced by the engine — see engine-spec.md.)

## Cell dimensions (the canonical reference — do the math, don't eyeball)

The 43″ 1080p 16:9 panel is **37.5″ × 21.1″ (952mm × 535mm)**, ~51 PPI (≈2 px/mm). On the 4×3 grid that makes each tile size physically:

| Size | px | physical | rough analog |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1×1 | 480 × 360 | 238mm × 178mm (9.4″ × 7.0″) | bigger than a postcard; ~half a sheet of paper |
| 1×2 / 2×1 | 480×720 / 960×360 | 238×356mm / 476×178mm | — |
| 2×2 | 960 × 720 | 476mm × 356mm (18.7″ × 14.0″) | ≈ a 23″ display |
| 4×3 | 1920 × 1080 | full panel | the whole Wall |

**The capacity rule (use this, not intuition):** a comfortable across-the-room list row is ~16–18mm tall (≈32–36px). So a **1×1 holds ~9–10 list rows** before header/padding; a **2×2 holds ~20**. A full week (7 days, or ~7–14 events) fits in a 1×1 with room to spare. When sizing any list/readout, compute against these numbers — the cells are far bigger than they feel in a chat mockup.

## Why 4×3

- Big enough to read and touch across a room. At 4 columns, each 1×1 tile is large (~235mm across on the 43″ panel — bigger than a postcard); a thin display number fills it and reads from the couch. A **2×2 is therefore ~476mm × 356mm (≈ a 23″ display)** — large enough that a dense, well-organized readout (the Weather Station face) is fully legible at distance. Going 6–8 columns shrinks cells to where thin type loses legibility at distance and walk-by touch targets get fiddly.
- Clean span math. 4 divides evenly by 1×1 / 1×2 / 2×2 — no orphan columns. A 2×2 is exactly a quadrant; a 1×2 is exactly half a row; 4×3 is the whole grid.
- Lands the centerpiece. Cameras as 2×2 = a clean quadrant, the natural "watched" size, with room for ~4 glance tiles + Home around it.
- Tension, accepted on purpose: 4×3 caps the on-screen count at ~4 distinct tiles.

## Per-tile native sizes

Each tile declares its own native sizes — the subset of {1×1, 1×2, 2×2, 2x3, 4×3} that that tile supports. Not every tile offers all five; a clock or climate tile has no business being 4×3.

- Most tiles = 2 native sizes, so resizing is trivial — tap toggles between the two, no picker, no ambiguity.
- Camera = the one tile with all 4 sizes (1×1 single feed → 2×2 grid → 4×3 full-screen w/ PTZ). It's the centerpiece, earns the complexity, and contains the expensive multi-size work to one tile.
- Each tile effectively carries a closed size (how it sits normally) and an open size (what tap/event grows it to).

### Size notation: cols × rows (W×H), spec-wide

Full-Wall = 4×3; chess open = 3×2; a domino open = 2×1. Read every size in this file as Width×Height.

### Future-add rule: 12 tiles is the working ceiling

Twelve keeps a regenerated stage exactly full. Add a 13th capability by folding it into an existing tile as a face (faces-as-a-list makes this cheap — Settings folds into Home, Chess into Games, Facts into Photos, Movies into Media, Hurricane + Radar + the Weather Station all fold into Weather), not by overflowing the library. The drawer can technically scroll past one screen, so a 13th wouldn't break the layout — but "fold, don't add" still holds and is preferred.

## The stage, the list, and pull-to-regenerate

Browsing the whole library and resetting the deck are two separate things.

- See everything = scroll down. The stage shows the calm subset; every other tile is a 1×1 in the drawer directly below. Drag/scroll down to browse the full library. The scroll snaps to whole 4×3 screens, so it never parks halfway through a tile.
- Jump to a tile = tap it, on the stage or in the drawer. Tapping a tile down in the drawer promotes it onto the stage as a closed 1×1 — it does not open it. It animates upward toward the stage; your scroll position stays put, so you scroll back up to it. If the stage is full of open tiles, the oldest open tile shrinks to 1×1 to make room. Tap it again, now on the stage, to open it. (A tile already on the stage opens on the first tap.) There is no separate "selector tap."
- Reset / start fresh = pull-to-regenerate. A rubber-band pull DOWN that STARTS in the dark gutter (not on a card) while you're at the very top closes every open tile, returning all 12 to 1×1 on the stage. Gutter-only so it can't collide with an on-tile face-swipe or a list-scroll; pull past the threshold and release to fire, or let go short and it springs back. The bottom edge does nothing — it's just the end of the drawer scroll.
- Stage return. Leave the panel scrolled into the drawer and, after the 30-min idle window, the view glides back to the stage on its own.
- Reset-to-default lives on the shuffle / manual-cascade control (Settings); pull-to-regenerate is "close everything to 1×1," not "snap to a saved default."

Why this shape: "show everything" is just scrolling (discoverable, no hidden gesture), and the only thing left to gesture for is the reset — and a pull-from-top is the universal "refresh" idiom. There is no collapse → exact-restore snapshot, because the library is always visible; regenerate is a deliberate clean slate, not an undo.

Last resort gestures: pinch/spread (multi-touch reliability varies wildly by commercial panel — an almost-working gesture is worse than none) and a grid-icon button (a floating/fixed bright element is a burn-in patch and anti-Metro).

Integrator note: the pull is the sole reset gesture; the always-visible drawer is the backstop for "I just want to find a tile." HA Sections edit mode is the separate path for deliberate rearranging (drag/add/remove/resize). Phase-C touch checks: a vertical drag starting on a tile must scroll natively (not be eaten by the swipe), and the gutter pull must be finger-reachable.

## How a tile changes size

### Three triggers — fully separated

1. **Tap (use mode)** — human, deliberate, constant. Tapping a tile grows it from placed → open size on the stage, and the stage reflows to fit. Open is additive + sticky: tapping a second tile opens it alongside the first; tapping an open tile closes it. Already-open tiles stay put unless packing genuinely needs them to move; the new tile grows from the slot nearest your finger. When the stage can't fit another open tile, the oldest open tile demotes to 1×1 (never the tile you just tapped). A tap on a tile currently in the DRAWER first promotes it to a 1×1 on the stage — not open — then a second tap opens it (see the drawer section). If a tile's placed size already equals its open size, tap enters the tile's content/app mode (e.g. the camera tap-path), so tap always does something.
2. **Event** — system, automatic. On a doorbell/person event (or alert) the relevant tile auto-grows to its open size and reflows the Wall, with no tap and no picker — gated by idle vs. engaged (below).

Triggers 1 and 2 produce an identical end state, so the person can't tell which path got them there — which is what makes it feel coherent.

### Reflow

When any tile grows or shrinks — from any trigger — the other tiles repack to fit, and restore when it shrinks back. This is a real packing behavior, not show/hide on fixed positions, and it is the load-bearing custom work of the project. It replaces the old fixed "event slot." (How it animates — FLIP / GPU transform — and the packing algorithm live in engine-spec.md.)

### Event behavior

- Manual tiles — place and stay. Most of the library. Drop them, they sit. No event logic. (Markets, Maps, Photos, Calendar, etc.)
- Event-borrow (e.g. doorbell camera): auto-opens on the event + a short tail (~2 min), then reflows back. Nothing is permanently displaced.
- Event-window (e.g. game-day takeover, severe-weather alert): grows for the duration of the window, then leaves.

### The size ladder + colored-frame back-button

The rule every multi-size tile follows:

- A tile has an ordered list of sizes (the ladder), smallest first. The smallest is the closed/placed size; each tap on content climbs one rung. Most tiles are 2-rung (1x1 → open); Cameras is the 3-rung case (1x1 → 2x2 → 4x3). Max 3 rungs by design unless otherwise changed.
- Going back is always one rung down, never a jump to 1×1. From the top of a 3-rung tile you step to the middle rung.
- The colored FRAME is the universal back-button AND the tile's identity. Every open tile wears a band in its category colour; tapping it steps back one rung. The hit zone is wider than the visible band (mis-tap guard). At the closed 1×1 the frame is thin/decorative — a tiny tile shouldn't spend pixels on a back band with nothing to go back to. This solves the "full-bleed face has no empty space to tap" problem.
- At the top rung, a content-tap also steps back — unless the face claims its own tap (a full-screen camera's pause/PTZ controls own their taps; a face may intercept before the generic step), or the tile opts out of content-tap-back entirely. **Home is the opt-out:** its Quick-Settings content taps are inert (only the colored frame backs out), and the 4×3 Admin is reachable only through the 7-tap gear — never by climbing from a content tap. (The engine flags → engine-spec.)
- At full-screen 4×3 there is no gutter, so pull-to-regenerate can't fire and there's no card-free background to start it on — the colored frame is the only way out. Intentional.

### Camera tap-path (as built)

The camera's multi-size interaction. Tap grows the tile through its sizes; swipe rotates feeds within a size; a tap on empty/ambient space or the frame backs out.

- 1×1 — **flips driveway ↔ front door** (Clear feeds — the Fluent split was dropped; Clear at every size), swipe to rotate (idle auto-flip too). The corner tag names the current feed. Tap → 2×2.
- 2×2 — a quadrant grid. **Top two quadrants are the live feeds** (driveway top-left, front door top-right — both Clear). **Bottom two quadrants are the daily activity pair (BUILT, LIVE):** bottom-left = the snapshot **reel** (newest-first stills crossfading on ~4 s), bottom-right = the **event list** (scrollable, full window). A **filter chip bar** (Person / Vehicle / Pet, single-select, tap-again clears) drives BOTH — reel and list always show the same filtered set.
  - **Tap a live feed quadrant → that feed goes 4×3 full-screen** (per-quadrant jump — the tap routes through the engine's `ctx.openRung`, opening straight onto the tapped feed's face, not a generic open-to-driveway).
  - **Tap the reel → opens the 4×3 snapshot viewer** (below), landing on whatever the reel was showing.
  - **Tap a list row → jumps the reel to that snapshot + pauses its auto-cycle for 30 s**, then resumes.
  - Tap the frame → backs out.
- 4×3 — **three faces**, two reachable via the live-feed flip and one tap-only:
  - **cameraFull ↔ cameraFullFront** (the two live feeds) flip full-screen — swipe rotates between THESE TWO only (the swipe walks past the viewer). The corner tag names the current feed.
  - **cameraSnapshots (the snapshot viewer)** — the tap-only review face, reached ONLY by tapping the 2×2 reel. Layout: a big still on the LEFT (~74%, `object-fit: contain`) + a narrow event **rail** on the right (~26%, same filter chips + scrollable list as the 2×2, the SHOWN row marked with a left-edge accent + lit background). **Horizontal swipe on the big still = prev/next through the filtered snapshot list** — drag-follows the finger, commits past ~1/3 of a swipe (mirrors the 1×1 live-feed slide verbatim), wraps both directions. A rail row tap jumps the big image to that event (instant, no animation). A filter chip tap resets the viewer to the newest of the new filter. The frame band steps back to 2×2 like any open tile. Stills only (no decode); real CLIP playback is the deferred Frigate upgrade (mini-PC gated).
  - **PTZ overlay (step 4b), driveway-feed face only.** A square button at the feed's bottom-right blooms (scale/opacity, transform-only) into a transparent in-feed panel — preset positions on top (read live from the camera's saved presets) · a pan/tilt d-pad (press-and-hold to move, release to stop) · zoom −/+ (press-hold) — and a tap anywhere outside the panel closes it. - Future 3rd/4th camera — claims a feed in the 2×2 top row + joins the 1×1 and 4×3 feed-flip lists. The model doesn't change.

**Motion + feeds (as built):** the feeds live on a **persistent engine video layer** that is never torn down, so opening/flipping/growing has **no cold-start black beat** (the cure for the old per-render rebuild). The camera flip is therefore NOT the Wall's 3D page-turn — a rotating, reparented `<video>` is the one motion the compositor can't keep clean — it's a **slide or crossfade** instead (the auto idle-flip picks between them; a manual swipe is a directional slide). On a resize the feeds **window-FLIP**: each warm feed morphs from its old rect to its new one in lockstep with the tile (transform/opacity only), so a feed grows/shrinks smoothly with no snap. (An earlier "freeze the feed to a still during a flip" mechanism CAUSED the jank it was meant to prevent — the mid-flip `setConfig` rebuilt the card right when the flip needed the frame budget — and was removed; a decoding `<video>` riding a pure transform is fine, the GPU compositor just translates the layer.) (Which stream feeds which size + the layer mechanics — stream-swap-by-size, the persistent layer — are tiles-spec/engine, not design.)

### Faces can depend on size

Which faces a tile rotates through can differ by its current size, with a fallback: a size that declares no list of its own uses the closed-size list (so you only specify overrides where a larger size genuinely shows different content). Weather is the example: 1×1 flips current↔tomorrow; 2×2 cycles weekly → radar → station.

## The open-face toolkit

The menu of tools every card draws from when its open face is designed — deliberately not the per-card designs (those are decided when each card is built against real data; parked — see Open questions).

- Two-block open model is universal. A 1×1 (placed) face is one block. Opening adds a second block. Orientation is just how the two blocks sit: 1×2 portrait = block 2 below block 1; 2×1 landscape = block 2 beside block 1. Same content either way — the renderer swaps the flex/grid direction. (Example: Media = album-art block + transport-controls block, stacked or side-by-side.) (Multi-zone faces like the Weather Station console are a richer case of the same idea — fixed sub-regions, no drill-in.)
- This makes 1×2 tiles 2×1-capable by construction — the orientation-flex lever, grounded. A block-pair has no inherent axis, so the packer can render the tile in whichever orientation fits (more valid arrangements before it must bump an open tile; sidesteps the 4×3 middle-row trap). A card may still have a natural default orientation; the other stays available for packing.
- A block may contain a scrollable list — not a separate archetype, just what a block can hold (Settings' notification history is a block-with-a-list at 2×2).
- No block-level drill-in. No back buttons. Tapping inside a block never toggles orientation or opens a sub-navigation layer. If tapping data must reveal more, the card grows to its next open size via the normal tap-to-open + reflow path, and backing out reuses the camera tap-empty-to-back pattern.
- No visible navigation controls anywhere. The entire model is tap/swipe-intuitive — no back arrows, no grid buttons, no pin glyphs, no on-face chrome. Visible controls are bright static elements (burn-in patches that can't drift) and are anti-Metro. Every "go deeper / back out / change face / hold still" need is met by a gesture.
- Persistent-priority ≠ a face control. "Always keep the clock visible" is a priority concern (resist being dropped/cascaded), a registry setting — not a pin button on the face. Temporary stillness is handled by touch (the idle timer).

## Swipe to rotate faces

The manual trigger for tile content rotation (System 2). Pairs with the auto idle-flip — same machinery, two triggers (exactly like tap-vs-event for reflow).

- Swipe left / right on a tile = advance / reverse through that tile's faces list. N=2 → flip; N≥3 → step through the cycle (Weather's 2×2 is N=3: weekly → radar → station).
- **Vertical swipe is normally the drawer scroll**, but an open tile's face MAY claim vertical drags for its own paging — the face wins over drawer scroll while open (the engaged-open state; same disambiguation seam the scroll-list uses). First user: **Calendar's open week-box view** pages weeks on a vertical swipe (snap to prev/next). This is paging *within* a face, distinct from the horizontal face-flip — a face can use both axes (Calendar: vertical = page weeks, horizontal = flip to day-detail).
- **A face MAY likewise claim HORIZONTAL drags inside a sub-region** for a continuous-pointer readout, in which case the face-flip is reached by swiping elsewhere on the face. First (and so far only) user: **Markets' chart console** — the plot area owns horizontal drags for the always-on scrub cursor, so the flip to the Overview grid is a swipe on the header/controls strip, not across the plot. (This is the Wall's first non-tap continuous-pointer interaction; the engine swallows the post-drag click so a scrub never closes the tile — see engine-spec.md.)
- Swipe ALWAYS animates — a swipe is never a dead gesture. On a 1-face tile the swipe flips the tile to itself (animation plays, same content returns) — a no-action swipe feels broken, and this costs nothing (a cycle-of-length-1).
- Distinguished from scroll/pull by axis. Horizontal-on-a-tile = rotate faces; vertical = native drawer scroll (or, in the gutter at the top, pull-to-regenerate). The diagonal wedge does nothing.
- The manual flip is a true two-faced 180° flip that follows the finger — the whole tile turns, the current face rotating away as the next face rotates in, spinning in the swipe direction. Deliberately not the random H/V/slide/crossfade the auto idle-flip picks — a deliberate gesture wants one consistent motion that tracks the hand; the ambient auto-flip keeps its desync'd variety. (Flip + swipe implementation → engine-spec.md.)
- A manual swipe trips the 30-min idle-protection timer (the same clock a tap uses), so swiping a tile is also the manual "hold this still" — it holds the tile out of cascade for 30 min, then it rejoins ambient. Touch = temporary stillness; no visible pin needed.

### Scope hedge

Not every tile needs all its sizes polished on day one. Build each tile's placed + open size well first; let any other native sizes be functional-not-fancy until they matter.

## Tile motion — the animation roster

The Wall has three distinct motion systems, each with its own trigger, simultaneity rule, and scheduler. Keeping them separate is load-bearing — they have opposite rules and would fight if jammed into one. This is the design menu; the engine implements them.

Provenance (real Windows Phone Metro motion): Metro had two families — what individual tiles did (Live Tile flip/cycle/peek) and what the whole Start screen did (staggered entrance, turnstile, page-turns). The crucial detail we steal: Metro's tile flips fired at random, desynchronized intervals with "smart logic to make them asynchronous," and the system animated transitions "in harmony" — several tiles gently in motion at once, but never synchronized, so the screen always had life without looking like a glitch. Metro also ran its animations on the compositor thread — exactly our transform/opacity discipline. We're reproducing a proven recipe.

### System 1 — Reflow (resize + repack) — the engine keystone

A tile grows/shrinks (tap, event, or edit) and the whole stage repacks around it. Simultaneity rule: one trigger at a time — you tap one tile, or one event fires, and the tiles moving aside aren't independent animations, they're all part of resolving that single layout change. Scheduler: event-driven.

### System 2 — Tile content rotation (flip-to-reveal) — UX feature

A tile cycles its own content in place, without moving position. Faces are a list of length N (N≥1), and every tile rotates through its faces — one mechanism, three cases by N:

- N=1 — persistent. Nothing different to rotate to (Home clock). A card declares a faces list; persistent is a list of one — not written twice, no padding.
- N=2 — flip. Front↔back (Weather current↔tomorrow; Sports next-game↔last-result; Photos slideshow↔facts).
- N≥3 — cycle. Multiple in-season sports; Weather's 2×2 (weekly → radar → station).

There's no "does this tile flip?" flag — the engine always rotates the list; how many faces a card defines is the only knob (a per-card decision parked to card-build). Manual trigger = swipe (above). Simultaneity rule: multiple tiles at once, but staggered and desynced — the "2 or 3 moving at a time" Metro feel. Never fire flips in unison. Scheduler: timer-driven with a desync/stagger algorithm. The stagger is also a frame-budget tool (simultaneous flips = simultaneous re-renders competing for one frame). Idle-only (frozen by engagement).

### System 3 — System choreography (entrance) — the wow layer

Whole-grid sequenced motion: a staggered entrance / fly-in on wake-from-screen-off — tiles cascade in one-after-another rather than snapping on (Metro's most recognizable flourish; cheap staggered transform+opacity; animates the stage, the drawer sits out). Simultaneity rule: many tiles, sequenced/cascading (a wave, not a block). Scheduler: one-shot, triggered by wake. Built last — cheap, high-impact, depends on nothing.

### Peek (slide-off-to-reveal)

Metro's "peek," our version. A tile's face is a TWO-LAYER construct: the opaque colored face (icon + category-colour background) sits ON TOP of a static image underneath. Peek = the colored top layer **rubber-band drags vertically** off its own backing to reveal the static image beneath, stops at **~65–75% open** (never fully off — a glimpse, not a face change), **hangs a beat**, then **bounces shut** (elastic retract). The top layer never leaves; it slides and snaps back. This is distinct from a flip (no axis rotation) and from a slide-reveal-from-edge (the layer moves off its OWN backing, it isn't a separate element entering).

Two uses of the one mechanic:

- **Launcher idle motion (Earth/Maps — the only peek tile).** A launcher tile's faces have no live data, so peek is what gives them idle life. Each Honey face's icon+background layer peeks down to flash the static image underneath (Maps face → a map still; Earth face → a globe still — face-specific images), then bounces shut. Fires **randomly, idle-only, and ONLY when the tile is not mid-flip** — peek and flip are mutually exclusive on a given tile (a peek + a flip at once would tear). It rides the same L2 idle scheduler as flip-to-reveal and obeys the same engagement freeze. The tile's normal N=2 flip (Maps↔Earth, manual swipe + auto) is unchanged and is how the user picks which app to launch; peek is additive, occupying the still gaps between flips. **Status: BUILT.** The slide-down/hang/elastic-retract motion + the flip mutual-exclusion are live in the engine; the two-layer DOM (Honey cover over the still) is in faces/maps.js. **Maps/Earth is the ONLY tile that peeks** — it's an opt-in (a face must tag its cover `data-peek-layer`, and only the launcher does), so no other tile can peek by accident. Remaining: the two stills (>>>[OWNER]<<< drops them); until then a peek reveals a darker-Honey panel so the motion is still visible.
- **Notification flavor (later).** The same slide-off-to-reveal as a softer announce than the banner (Markets flashing a big move, Calendar the next event). Optional; layered in once the core systems work.

Static images are bundled local assets (`/config/www`) — one map, one globe — never a live snapshot, so the launcher keeps its no-API/no-cloud-key principle. The peek is transform-only (GPU compositor), on the 16ms budget. (Engine: a new L2 motion variant + the two-layer face construct + scheduler mutual-exclusion with flip — see engine-spec.md.)

### Home flash (the clock's celestial reveal)

The Home 1×1's one bespoke motion. Home rests on its clock face (N1); on its ambient tick it does a quick **reveal-and-return** to the celestial "The >>>[FAMILY_NAME]<<<'s" face (N2) — flip / slide / crossfade / peek, **any** motion in the stack picked at random (Photos' "every animation we have" approach) — **holds ~2s, then returns to the clock**. Unlike flip-to-reveal, N2 is never left sitting there: it's a flash, so the clock is on screen ~99% of the time. Idle-only, transform-only, and it rides the same L2 scheduler + concurrency cap + flip/peek mutual-exclusion as Peek (it's a reveal-and-return cousin). Cadence is an Admin → Motion slider (the helper + engine read are wired; the slider UI lands with Admin). **Status: BUILT** — v1 is a crossfade reveal-and-return on its own slower cadence window; the random-motion variety (slide/flip/peek) is a later polish. (Engine: a Home-only flash variant reusing the Peek reveal-and-return plumbing — see engine-spec.md.)

### Cascade drift (position re-pack) — the design's burn-in contribution

The whole stage re-packs into new positions on a slow cycle so no screen region stays lit the same way all day. This is real burn-in mitigation (moving pixels, not just content). Multi-cell tiles (a 2×2 camera) move as a unit.

- Cadence: one shuffle every ~30–60 min, never every few seconds.
- Activity guard: never fire while someone is touching/present; defer until idle.
- Manual trigger: a "shuffle" control (Admin → Motion, or the pull-to-regenerate gutter gesture) fires it on demand and doubles as a burn-in reset.
- Honest correction: content rotation in fixed boxes (flip-to-reveal) is a UX feature, not burn-in protection — only moving position (or the screen turning off) protects pixels. (The full burn-in stack — panel choice, scheduled screen-off, the overnight scrim — lives in family-dashboard-spec.md. Cascade is the Wall's slice of it.)

(The engine wiring is in engine-spec.md.)

## Interaction model: motion is an idle-only behavior

The governing principle, borrowed from Windows Phone: the system only animates when no one is engaged. Engagement is the off-switch for motion.

### The three rules

0. Reflow is not "ambient motion." The Wall reflowing when you tap a tile, or when an event auto-opens while idle, is the system responding to a trigger — not moving on its own. The idle-only rules govern ambient motion (cascade drift, auto content-flips), which the Wall does unprompted. The line: the Wall never moves on its own while you're engaged — but it always moves in response to your touch.
1. Touch freezes ambient motion. Any touch — or presence within reach — pauses all auto-flips and cascade for a couple of minutes. If you're close enough to care that something drifted, you're close enough to have already frozen it. Kills ~90% of "it moved while I was looking."
2. An opened tile is in "app mode" — but only while you're engaged. Open is a set; every tile in it gets app-mode treatment (no auto-flip). Two idle clocks: an open tile is cascade-protected only for a 30-minute open-tile idle window since last touch; after that it rejoins the ambient pool and can be cascaded (moved as a unit, keeping its size). And events always win once past the 2-minute ambient gate — if you've been idle 2+ min and a person or vehicle is classified on a camera, the camera auto-opens even if another tile was left open. Rationale: real events must surface, a forgotten-open tile shouldn't block that, and an abandoned-open tile is exactly the biggest lit region burn-in wants moved. 2 min governs ambient flips + event-vs-banner; 30 min is the extra grace before cascade relocates an open tile.
3. Events while engaged announce, they don't hijack. If someone is actively engaged, an incoming event does not silently replace what they're looking at — a banner appears ("Someone at the front door →") and the user taps to switch. The system informs, it does not override.

### Same event, two responses — switched by engagement

| Situation | Doorbell / person event behavior |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Nobody at the panel (idle) | Camera tile auto-opens and reflows the Wall — no tap needed |
| Someone actively using the panel | Banner appears; user taps to switch — no hijack |
| Someone watching a different camera | Banner over the current feed; tap to switch, or ignore and keep watching |

The instant someone is engaged, that same event downgrades to a banner. One event, two behaviors, gated by "is a human touching the panel right now."

### The "engaged?" signal

Two cheap inputs OR'd together: a last-touch timestamp (any tap in the last N minutes = engaged). engaged == true → suspend all ambient animations + downgrade event interrupts to banners. A handful of automations — but it is the load-bearing logic that makes the entire tile concept feel respectful instead of chaotic.

## Banner styling

The "announce, don't hijack" event banner (rule 3). Which events earn a banner is the notification policy in family-dashboard-spec.md; this is what one looks like.

Placement — bottom edge, full-width strip. Bottom is where the eye expects transient status (toast convention); a top strip would collide with the pull-to-regenerate gesture; bottom-anchored never overlaps the camera 2×2's top-row feeds. A corner toast is rejected (too small to read across a kitchen, worse burn-in spot than a briefly-shown strip).

Look — the Wall's language in strip form:

- Colour = a single **amber "event" ribbon** for every banner (the `--wall-event` token + its dark ink), uniform across all categories. **AS-BUILT decision (supersedes the old per-category-banner-colour plan):** one emphatic alert colour reads faster across a kitchen than a palette the family would have to learn, so the banner's category signal is the tracked-out **tag word** + message, not its colour. **Per-category colour lives only in the notification-log chips** (below), where rows sit still long enough to scan by colour.
- Type = Wall type — left-aligned, ragged-right. Short bold message in regular weight, optional tracked-out uppercase tag. Never centered.
- Hard rules hold — sharp corners, no shadow, no gradient, no rounded edges. A flat block sliding up from the bottom gutter — a tile that happens to be a strip.
- Animate in/out (slide up / slide down) so even its lit moments aren't identical pixels.

Behavior: the whole strip is the tap target → tap switches to the relevant tile/feed; ignore it and it ages out (tail = `wall_banner_tail_s`, default ~20s, kept short so the stack stays brief). **Banners STACK** (the family wants to never miss one): each new banner enters at the bottom gutter and pushes the stack **up**; the **oldest sits at the top and fades out first** as it ages; black-gutter spacing between strips so it reads as a short column of flat tiles. **Capped at 3 visible** — a burst beyond 3 isn't drawn (still in the household log), so the stack can't climb into the camera feeds; short tails clear it fast. The stack **settles** (slides to close gaps) on each expiry. The whole stack **floats above the grid as a Wall-level overlay layer — it never reflows or repositions the tiles** (transform/opacity only; no FLIP cost on the grid for a transient strip).

Two banner modes (AS-BUILT). The **engaged** path shows the **full** strip — ~20s life, a trailing tap-arrow, tap-to-open the target tile. A **takeover** (idle auto-open) instead fires a **trimmed label** — short life (~6s, `takeoverBannerMs`), **no arrow**, tap-to-dismiss — because the engine has already opened the target tile to its event face, so the strip is just a quick "this is what you're now looking at" label, not a call to action.

Every takeover follows one rule with no exceptions: auto-open when idle, downgrade to a silent banner when engaged, audio muted in quiet hours. (Severe weather used to be the lone engaged-override; that exception was dropped — someone actively using the Wall during an alert is an edge case not worth a special branch.)

## Pop-ups & confirm-to-act

No floating modals / dimmed-backdrop pop-ups anywhere — they are the anti-Metro pattern. A dialog hovering above a dimmed Wall violates the hard rules (shadows, rounded corners, floating layers) and the dimmed backdrop is a large static darkened region = burn-in. Banners are edge strips, tap-to-open grows tiles in-plane, the camera full-screen is a 4×3 tile — never a lightbox.

The one blocking interaction — confirm-to-act, required for security-sensitive controls (garage, locks, Nissan remote start). Built Metro-correct — a takeover panel, not a floating box:

- Replaces content in-plane (same flat block, sharp corners, no shadow, no dim-the-background scrim). It is a tile momentarily showing a question.
- Colour = stakes — high-consequence actions use the alert red.
- Type = Wall type — big question, two oversized tap targets (Open / Cancel), left-aligned ragged-right.
- Never auto-confirms (safety). Auto-cancels and reflows back after ~10–15s untouched, so a stray tap can't leave a live "Open garage" button sitting on the Wall.
- Modal in behavior (blocks until answered), not in appearance (no floating layer).

That's the whole trick: hard-stop semantics while staying flat and in-plane.

**The one exception — the Admin control room.** The no-floating-modal / no-dim-backdrop rule is a Metro/Wall rule; the **Admin 4×3 is explicitly off-design-language**, so inside it the **keyboard** (the existing two-mode `FloatingKeyboard` from chess — ABC for names, a **123 numpad** layer for numbers + ticker symbols `^ - . =`; no separate keypad, the numpad covers numeric entry) and Admin's destructive **Confirm** panels ARE floating over a dim scrim — deliberately, so Admin feels like a different, modern app. Each pad has an explicit **Enter** (commit) and **✕/Back** (cancel), and a **tap anywhere off the pad cancels** (the camera tap-to-back idiom). The exemption is scoped to Admin only and does NOT relax the frame budget (transform/opacity only, native scroll). Everywhere else on the Wall the hard rule holds.

## Theme

The Metro type-and-colour system. Governing principle (Swiss/International Typographic Style, which Metro descends from): type is the UI, colour blocks are containers, the canvas is dark. Get the type right and everything follows.

### Typography — the load-bearing layer

- Typeface — Lato. Free, loads locally, no API key — a humanist sans with a genuine thin weight, the closest free font to Segoe WP's feel that survives the wall. (Rejected: Roboto — mushy thin weight; Inter — cooler/more mechanical. Maximum-authenticity option if ever wanted: Selawik, Microsoft's free Segoe clone, but it's a manual font install — Lato wins on effort-for-result.)
- Display weight — 300, not the true hairline 100. The thin-large-number-vs-regular-small-label contrast is the Metro signature, but 100 fringes and shimmers on a 43″ panel across a room; 300 keeps the thin look while staying crisp at distance.
- Weight contrast is the signature — thin-300 display number against regular-400 labels. That contrast, not the colour, is what reads as "Metro."
- Letterspacing — tight on big display text; tracked-out (~+7%) uppercase on small labels.
- Alignment — left-aligned, ragged-right, never centered/justified.

### Colour & palette — the Master-Hue system (as built)

Colour has two layers now: a **category default** (theme file) and a **live Master Hue** (the Appearance picker) that overrides it. themes/the-wall.yaml stays the boot default; the engine\'s palette subsystem is authoritative when a hue is picked.

- Base: canvas #181410, surface #241E17, ink/cream #EDE3D2, ink-dark #181410, ink-muted #9C9180. Canvas stays near-black for burn-in + the dark living room (never a white background).
- **Master Hue (BUILT — the headline colour control).** The owner picks ONE colour in the Control Room → Appearance tab and the WHOLE board recolours to a *scatter* of shades of that hue (the reference look: one colour family, deep→bright across the tiles, never a flat gradient and never the same tile always darkest). Picked from a ~52-swatch grid (4 columns) spanning the full spectrum — reds→oranges→yellows→greens→teals→blues→purples→magentas, plus a dedicated **earthy** band (clay/sienna/ochre/wheat/khaki/sage/taupe/cocoa) and **neutrals** (charcoal/slate/fjord/stone/fog). Every swatch carries its own lightness, so there are genuine DARK picks (Maroon/Navy/Forest/Pine/Plum/Espresso) and LIGHT picks (Blush/Mint/Sky/Lavender/Wheat/Fog), not hue micro-steps at one lightness. The board scatter biases toward the picked hue\'s own lightness (a dark pick darkens the whole board, a light pick lightens it) while keeping tonal spread for variety. **Re-tapping the active colour re-shuffles** the scatter (a fresh random seed); **Default** clears the override back to the theme palette. The pick lives in `input_text.wall_palette` ("<hueKey>|<seed>") and applies at runtime with no HA restart.
- **Settings stays charcoal.** The Home/Settings tile is excluded from the Master Hue (its neutral charcoal keeps the control surface always findable). The Control Room\'s back-border band, however, DOES track the master hue (a deep saturated shade of it frames the charcoal CR).
- **Banners stay marigold.** The warning-ribbon colour (`--wall-event`, amber #fbb333) is deliberately EXEMPT from the Master Hue — a warning must stand out, not blend into the board. Live event banners set their own override colour (severe weather = alert red, etc.).
- **THE CONTRAST ENGINE (BUILT — one readability authority for ALL text/glyph/tone).** Every coloured-text decision routes through one engine (engine/palette.js) so nothing is "readable only because the tile happened to be dark." It measures real WCAG contrast against each tile\'s actual shade and returns: **ink** (cream vs near-black, AA 4.5:1 for text / relaxed 3:1 for large hero numbers + decorative glyphs), the **tag** colour (the tracked-out corner stamp), the **detail/subtext** colour, and the **semantic tones**. This SUPERSEDES the old "auto-luminance rejected, keep taste in the hexes" call — taste lives in the hue picks; the engine guarantees the text on top is always readable on whatever shade a tile landed on.
- **Semantic tones are shade-aware but meaning-fixed.** Markets up/green & down/red, climate warm/cool, alert red: the engine keeps each tone\'s HUE fixed (red always = down, green always = up) and only corrects its LIGHTNESS per tile, so the figure reads on any shade while staying unmistakably itself. (Previously a fixed 6-hex `chipTones` table, blind to both the master hue and the tile shade.)
- **Calendar person colours track the master hue.** >>>[OWNER]<<< & >>>[PARTNER]<<< were fixed teal/amber identity hexes; now they\'re two shades of the master hue forced to OPPOSITE ends of the tonal spread (deep vs bright) so they stay distinct, contrast-aware on whatever tile they sit. They fall back to the legacy teal/amber when no master hue is active. (Calendar uses `--cal-person-N` vars the engine sets, never baked hexes.)
- **Identity colours stay TRUE (exempt, by design).** Sports team colours (live team data) and the SPC severe-risk chip (1–5 = the fixed external SPC standard, 0 = neutral grey) are identity, not decoration — they never pass through the Master Hue or the contrast engine. Documented in code so a future pass doesn\'t "fix" them. (Radar map pins, chess pieces, and media provider badges sit on their own imagery/badges with built-in halos and are likewise left as contrast-safe-on-their-own-backdrop.)
- **Per-category ink pairing (the theme-default layer, still true).** When NO master hue is active, the theme\'s s27 palette shows: each category ships wall-X + wall-X-ink + wall-X-tint set together, so text colour follows the block automatically. The Master Hue replaces this set at runtime with engine-generated, contrast-corrected values.

Severe weather = a clear alert red. **Mechanism BUILT — the per-face `colorFor(data)` engine seam (see engine-spec).** Sports\' **Saints faces** are the first user (they recolour the tile to neutral charcoal); the event-state overrides (doorbell / severe weather) reuse the same seam when the event wiring lands. - **As-built category split (live theme).** Maps/Earth and Media were given their **own** category colours, split off from the shared Data and Family blocks: `wall-maps` (Terracotta `#ba624e`) and `wall-media` (Steel Blue `#4b74a6`), both deep blocks → cream ink. These live in `themes/the-wall.yaml`. ### Canvas & text treatments

- Corner category tag — uppercase, tracked-out, a dark shade of the tile's own colour (or light grey on the neutral charcoal tiles, where a dark shade vanished). Reads as a stamp.
- Big display number — cream/white, Lato 300.
- Secondary line (e.g. "Heat · set 70°") — a light tint of the tile's own colour (on-brand, readable at distance). A dark shade was tested here and rejected — it disappears into the block across the room.

### Hard rules (no exceptions)

- No borders, drop shadows, gradients, or rounded corners (Metro is sharp-cornered).
- Tiles are separated only by a few px of black gutter — no dividers, no frames.

### Theme implementation + glance-tile pattern (as-built)

The theme is a real HA theme (config/themes/the-wall.yaml): it sets the canvas/shape/colour globals + the hard rules (border-radius:0, no shadow, no border) and the wall-* category palette vars (with -ink/-tint shades) that card-mod and the engine pull per-tile. The one load-bearing gotcha: a @font-face declared in the main document does not cross into a card's shadow DOM, so Lato is injected via a card-mod card-mod-root @import in the theme — that's what reaches the tiles; without it, tiles silently fall back to default sans even though the font is globally loaded.

The interim simple tiles use a reusable glance-tile pattern: custom:button-card laid out with grid-template-areas (Mushroom was rejected — it centers content and fights the bottom-anchored Metro block). The grid skeleton is "tag glyph" / "temp temp" / "detail detail" (cols 1fr auto, rows auto 1fr auto), pinning four fields to their corners: tag (tracked-out uppercase, dark shade of the tile colour), glyph (state-driven MDI icon, white, top-right), the big value (Lato-300, bottom-left), and detail (tint of the tile colour, under the number). Tap action: none (no rejected more-info modal). These interim button-card tiles are being replaced face-by-face by the engine card; the visual recipe is what carries over.

## Performance — the override lens

Performance is not a fourth perspective alongside GUI / engineer / integrator — it's the constraint that overrides all three when they conflict. The GUI hat wants the gorgeous entrance; the integrator hat wants four feeds live at once; the engineer hat wants clean abstractions. Performance is the tiebreaker: not if it drops a frame on the box. The bar is "buttery in production" — not "buttery in this VM" (which lies), and not "buttery in theory" (until measured on real hardware in Phase C).

- The budget: 60fps = ~16ms/frame. Every animation finishes inside that or it stutters. The whole transform/opacity discipline exists to stay off the main thread and never blow it.
- It already drove half our settled calls: the i5-1235U's 80-EU iGPU over the N100/N305 (compositor headroom), transform/opacity-only, stream-swap-by-size, smaller streams at small sizes, and the radar face as a self-hosted flat-PNG opacity-crossfade loop rather than a live slippy-map (which would be a frame-budget risk on a moving/counter-scaling tile).
- The three risks it watches: (1) hass re-renders — HA pushes device state constantly; a naive card re-renders the whole Wall on every push, and one landing mid-animation is jank. Defense: a tile reads only its own entities and re-renders only its own face, never the grid. (2) Many live faces animating at once — pure-compositor transforms are cheap, but each live face updating mid-move competes for the 16ms; this is the real reason flip-to-reveal must stagger/desync. (3) Camera feed during motion — a decoding stream on a moving tile is the single heaviest case; it's why Cameras is the deliberate last face port.
- The golden rule: the VM proves correctness; the box + Phase C soak test proves smoothness. Never declare "buttery" won until it's measured on the real panel under real load.
